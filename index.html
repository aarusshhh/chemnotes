Chapter 1: Some Basic Concepts in Chemistry

  • Mole Concept: - 1 mole = \(6.022 \times 10^{23}\) entities (Avogadro’s constant). - Moles (\(n\)) = \(\frac{\text{Mass (m)}}{\text{Molar Mass (M)}}\).
  • Basic Laws of Chemistry:
    • Conservation of Mass: Total mass of reactants = total mass of products.
    • Definite Proportions: A compound has fixed mass ratios of elements.
    • Multiple Proportions: Elements combine in simple integer ratios in different compounds.
    • Reciprocal Proportions: Mass ratios of two elements reacting with a third are inversely proportional to their mutual reaction ratios.
  • Concentration Terms:
    • Molarity (M): \(\frac{\text{Moles of solute}}{\text{Volume of solution (L)}}\).
    • Molality (m): \(\frac{\text{Moles of solute}}{\text{Mass of solvent (kg)}}\).
    • Normality (N): \(\frac{\text{Moles of equivalents}}{\text{Volume of solution (L)}}\).
    • Mole Fraction (\(\chi_i\)): \(\frac{\text{Moles of component }i}{\text{Total moles}}\).
  • Dilution Formula: \(M_1V_1 = M_2V_2\) (relates initial/final concentrations and volumes).
  • Limiting Reagent: Reactant with smallest \(\frac{\text{moles available}}{\text{stoichiometric coefficient}}\) (consumed first).
  • Percentage Yield: \(\% \text{ Yield} = \left(\frac{\text{Actual Yield}}{\text{Theoretical Yield}}\right) \times 100\).

Chapter 2: Structure of Atom

  • Subatomic Particles: - Electron (\(e^-\)): Mass \(9.109 \times 10^{-31}\) kg, charge \(-e\). - Proton (\(p^+\)): Mass \(1.6726 \times 10^{-27}\) kg, charge \(+e\). - Neutron (\(n^0\)): Mass \(1.6749 \times 10^{-27}\) kg, neutral.
  • Atomic Number vs. Mass Number: - Atomic number (\(Z\)): Number of protons (defines element). - Mass number (A): Protons + Neutrons (sum of \(Z\) and neutrons).
  • Bohr’s Model (Hydrogen-like ions): - Energy of nth orbit: \(E_n = -\frac{Z^2 \times 13.6 \, \text{eV}}{n^2}\) (quantized energy levels). - Radius of nth orbit: \(r_n = \frac{n^2 \times a_0}{Z}\) (\(a_0 = 0.529 \, \text{Å}\), Bohr radius).
  • de Broglie Wavelength: \(\lambda = \frac{h}{p}\) (\(p = mv\), momentum; describes wave nature of particles).
  • Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle: \(\Delta x \times \Delta p \geq \frac{h}{4\pi}\) (can’t measure position and momentum precisely).
  • Quantum Numbers & Principles:
    • Principal (\(n\)): Energy level (\(n = 1, 2, 3,...\)).
    • Azimuthal (\(l\)): Subshell shape (\(l = 0\) to \(n-1\); \(s, p, d, f\)).
    • Magnetic (\(m_l\)): Orbital orientation (\(-l\) to \(+l\)).
    • Spin (\(m_s\)): Electron spin (\(+\frac{1}{2}, -\frac{1}{2}\)).
    • Aufbau Principle: Electrons fill orbitals by increasing energy (\(n + l\) rule), starting from lowest energy.
    • Pauli Exclusion Principle: No two electrons share the same 4 quantum numbers; max 2 electrons per orbital (opposite spins).
    • Hund’s Rule: Electrons occupy degenerate orbitals (same energy) singly with parallel spins before pairing (maximizes stability).
  • Rydberg Formula (Hydrogen-like ions): \(\frac{1}{\lambda} = R_H Z^2 \left(\frac{1}{n_1^2} - \frac{1}{n_2^2}\right)\) (\(R_H = 1.097 \times 10^7 \, \text{m}^{-1}\); predicts spectral wavelengths).
  • Photoelectric Effect: - Electrons emitted when light (≥ threshold frequency) strikes metal. - Einstein’s Equation: \(KE_{\text{max}} = hf - \phi\) (\(\phi\): work function; \(KE_{\text{max}}\): max kinetic energy of emitted electrons).
  • Electron Configuration Notation: Uses noble gas cores (e.g., Na: [\(\text{Ne}\)] 3s¹) to simplify writing valence electrons.

Chapter 3: Classification of Elements and Periodicity

  • Modern Periodic Law: Properties of elements are periodic functions of their atomic numbers (\(Z\)).
  • Periodic Table Structure: - Periods (Rows): 7 periods (n=1 to n=7), based on valence shell (\(n\)). - Groups (Columns): 18 groups (1–18), elements with similar valence configurations. - Blocks:
    • s-block: Groups 1–2 (\(ns^1\) to \(ns^2\)).
    • p-block: Groups 13–18 (\(ns^2 np^0\) to \(ns^2 np^6\)).
    • d-block: Groups 3–12 (transition metals, \((n-1)d^{1-10} ns^2\)).
    • f-block: Lanthanides/Actinides (\((n-2)f^{1-14} (n-1)d^0-1 ns^2\)).
  • Periodic Trends:
    • Across Period (Left→Right): - Atomic Radius: Decreases (smaller shell, higher \(Z\)). - Ionization Enthalpy (IE): Increases (harder to remove electrons). - Electron Gain Enthalpy (EGE): Becomes more negative (easier to gain electrons). - Electronegativity (EN): Increases (stronger electron attraction). - Metallic Character: Decreases (elements become less metallic).
    • Down Group (Top→Bottom): - Atomic Radius: Increases (larger shells). - IE: Decreases (weaker nuclear attraction). - EGE: Becomes less negative (weaker electron attraction). - EN: Decreases (weaker bond electron attraction). - Metallic Character: Increases (elements become more metallic).
  • Key Groups (Main Groups):
    • Group 1 (Alkali Metals): \(ns^1\), highly reactive, +1 ions (e.g., Na, K).
    • Group 2 (Alkaline Earth Metals): \(ns^2\), reactive, +2 ions (e.g., Mg, Ca).
    • Group 17 (Halogens): \(ns^2 np^5\), highly reactive non-metals, -1 ions (e.g., Cl, Br).
    • Group 18 (Noble Gases): \(ns^2 np^6\) (except He: \(1s^2\)), inert, full valence shells (e.g., Ne, Ar).

Chapter 4: Chemical Bonding and Molecular Structure

  • Types of Chemical Bonds:
    • Ionic Bond: - Formation: Electron transfer from metal to non-metal, forming cations/anions bound by electrostatic forces. - Example: NaCl (Na⁺ and Cl⁻). - Properties: High melting/boiling points, brittle, conducts electricity when molten/dissolved.
    • Covalent Bond: - Formation: Electron sharing between atoms (typically non-metals). - Types:
      • Non-polar: Equal sharing (ΔEN ≤ 0.4), e.g., H₂, O₂.
      • Polar: Unequal sharing (ΔEN 0.4–1.7), e.g., H₂O, HCl.
      - Bonding Pairs: Single (σ), double (σ+π), triple (σ+2π) bonds.
    • Metallic Bond: - Formation: Delocalized electrons around metal cations. - Properties: High conductivity, malleable, ductile, lustrous. - Example: Cu, Fe.
    • Hydrogen Bond: - Dipole-dipole interaction where H bonds to N, O, or F. - Intermolecular: Between molecules (e.g., H₂O), stronger than regular dipole-dipole. - Intramolecular: Within a molecule (e.g., o-nitrophenol), forms rings. - Effects: Higher boiling point, viscosity, surface tension (e.g., water’s BP > H₂S).
  • Ionic Bonding:
    • Lattice Energy (\(U\)): Energy to separate 1 mole of gaseous ions from a solid. \(U \propto \frac{Z^+ Z^-}{r}\) (r: sum of ionic radii). Higher \(U\) → more stable. - Example: NaCl, \(U \approx 787\) kJ/mol.
    • Born-Haber Cycle: Thermodynamic cycle to calculate \(U\) using enthalpies of atomization, ionization, electron gain, and formation. - Formula: \(\Delta H_f = \Delta H_{sub} + \frac{1}{2}\Delta H_{bond} + IE + \Delta H_{eg} + U\) (signs depend on reaction direction).
    • Factors Influencing Ionic Character:
      • Electronegativity difference (ΔEN): Larger ΔEN → more ionic.
      • Cation size: Smaller/higher charge cation → higher charge density → more polarization (covalent).
      • Anion size: Larger/lower charge anion → more polarizable → more covalent.
  • Covalent Bonding:
    • Octet Rule: Atoms gain/lose/share electrons to complete valence shell (8 e⁻; 2 for H/He). Exceptions: BF₃ (incomplete), SF₆ (expanded), NO (odd e⁻).
    • Lewis Structures: Valence electrons as dots, bonds as lines. Steps: Total valence e⁻, central atom, bonds, lone pairs, check octet. - Example: CO₂ structure → O=C=O (each O has 2 lone pairs).
    • Valence Bond Theory (VBT): Bonding via orbital overlap (similar energy, orientation, size). - σ Bond: Head-on overlap, cylindrical symmetry (e.g., C-C single bond). - π Bond: Sideways overlap, lower energy (e.g., C=C has one π bond).
    • Molecular Orbital Theory (MOT): Atomic orbitals combine into bonding (σ, π) and antibonding (σ*, π*) MOs. - Bond Order (\(BO\)): \(BO = \frac{1}{2}(N_b - N_a)\) ( \(N_b\): bonding e⁻, \(N_a\): antibonding e⁻). - Example: O₂ (\(N_b=8, N_a=6\) → \(BO=2\)), paramagnetic (unpaired π* e⁻).
  • Bond Parameters:
    • Bond Length (r): Distance between nuclei. Depends on atomic size and BO (higher BO → shorter). - Example: C-C (154 pm), C=C (134 pm), C≡C (120 pm).
    • Bond Order (BO): Strength/length measure. Higher BO → stronger/shorter bond. - N₂ (BO=3) stronger than O₂ (BO=2).
    • Bond Dissociation Energy (BDE): Energy to break 1 mole of bonds. Stronger bonds (higher BO) → higher BDE. - Example: H-H (436 kJ/mol), O=O (498 kJ/mol).
    • Bond Angle: Angle between adjacent bonds. Minimized by VSEPR (lone pairs repel more). - Example: NH₃ (107°) < CH₄ (109.5°) due to lone pair.
  • Molecular Structure (VSEPR Theory): - Predicts shape by minimizing electron pair repulsion. Repulsion order: LP-LP > LP-BP > BP-BP. - AXₙEₘ Notation: A=central, X=bonded atoms, E=lone pairs. - Examples:
    • AX₄ (CH₄): Tetrahedral, 109.5°.
    • AX₃E (NH₃): Pyramidal, 107°.
    • AX₂E₂ (H₂O): Bent, 104.5°.
    • AX₅ (PCl₅): Trigonal bipyramidal, 90°/120°.
    • AX₆ (SF₆): Octahedral, 90°.
  • Hybridization: Mixing atomic orbitals for directional bonding.
    • sp: 1s + 1p → 2 orbitals (linear, 180°). Examples: BeCl₂, CO₂.
    • sp²: 1s + 2p → 3 orbitals (trigonal planar, 120°). Examples: BF₃, ethene (C₂H₄).
    • sp³: 1s + 3p → 4 orbitals (tetrahedral, 109.5°). Examples: CH₄, CCl₄.
    • sp³d: 1s + 3p + 1d → 5 orbitals (trigonal bipyramidal). Examples: PCl₅, SF₄ (see-saw with 1 LP).
    • sp³d²: 1s + 3p + 2d → 6 orbitals (octahedral). Examples: SF₆, [Fe(CN)₆]⁴⁻.
  • Covalent Character in Ionic Compounds: - Fajan’s Rules: Polarization of anion by cation increases covalent character. - Factors: Small/highly charged cation; large/anion with low charge. - Example: LiCl (small Li⁺) has more covalent character than NaCl.
  • Dipole Moment (μ): Vector measure of molecular polarity. μ = q × d (q: charge, d: bond distance).
    • Units: Debye (1 D = 3.336 × 10⁻³⁰ C·m).
    • Polar molecules (μ ≠ 0): HCl (1.03 D), H₂O (1.85 D).
    • Non-polar (μ = 0): CO₂, BF₃ (symmetric structures).

Chapter 7: Redox Reactions

  • Redox Reactions: Fundamentals: - Redox (Reduction-Oxidation) reactions involve electron transfer between species. - Oxidation: Loss of electrons (or increase in oxidation number). - Reduction: Gain of electrons (or decrease in oxidation number). - Oxidizing Agent: Substance that accepts electrons (gets reduced). - Reducing Agent: Substance that donates electrons (gets oxidized).
  • Oxidation Number (ON):
    • Rules for Assigning ON:
      1. ON of an element in its elemental form (e.g., O₂, Fe, H₂) is 0.
      2. ON of monovalent ions (e.g., Na⁺, K⁻, Cl⁻) is +1 or -1.
      3. Sum of ONs in a neutral molecule is 0; in an ion, equals its charge (e.g., NH₄⁺: sum = +1).
      4. ON of oxygen is -2 (except in peroxides, O₂²⁻, where ON = -1; or with F, e.g., OF₂, where ON = +2).
      5. ON of hydrogen is +1 (except in metal hydrides, e.g., NaH, where ON = -1).
      6. Group 1 elements (Li, Na, K) have ON +1 in compounds; Group 2 elements (Mg, Ca) have ON +2.
    • Examples of ON Calculation:
      • S in SO₂: x + 2(-2) = 0 → x = +4.
      • Fe in Fe₃O₄ (mixed oxide, FeO·Fe₂O₃): Fe in FeO = +2, Fe in Fe₂O₃ = +3. Overall, (2 + 2×3)/3 = 8/3 ≈ +2.67.
      • Mn in MnO₄⁻: x + 4(-2) = -1 → x = +7.
      • ON of Cl in ClO⁻: x + (-2) = -1 → x = +1.
  • Types of Redox Reactions:
    • Combination: Elements combine to form a compound. - Example: 2Mg(s) + O₂(g) → 2MgO(s) (Mg oxidized, O₂ reduced).
    • Decomposition: A compound breaks into elements or simpler compounds. - Example: 2HgO(s) → 2Hg(l) + O₂(g) (Hg²⁺ reduced to Hg, O²⁻ oxidized to O₂).
    • Displacement:
      • Single Displacement: More reactive element displaces a less reactive element from its compound. - Example: Zn(s) + CuSO₄(aq) → ZnSO₄(aq) + Cu(s) (Zn oxidized, Cu²⁺ reduced).
      • Double Displacement: Two elements interchange partners. Usually non-redox unless ON changes. - Non-redox Example: NaCl(aq) + AgNO₃(aq) → NaNO₃(aq) + AgCl(s). - Redox Example: Cl₂(g) + 2KBr(aq) → Br₂(l) + 2KCl(aq) (Cl₂ oxidizes Br⁻).
    • Disproportionation (Auto-Redox): A single species acts as both oxidizing and reducing agent (same element oxidized and reduced). - Example: Cl₂(g) + H₂O(l) → HCl(aq) + HClO(aq) (Cl in Cl₂: 0 → -1 (reduced) and +1 (oxidized)).
    • Comproportionation: Reverse of disproportionation; two species with same element in different ONs combine to form a single species. - Example: ClO⁻(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) → Cl₂(g) + 2OH⁻(aq) (Cl in +1 (ClO⁻) and -1 (Cl⁻) → 0 (Cl₂)).
  • Balancing Redox Reactions:
    • Oxidation Number Method:
      1. Assign oxidation numbers to all elements.
      2. Identify oxidized (ON ↑) and reduced (ON ↓) species.
      3. Calculate total electron loss (oxidation) and gain (reduction). Equalize these values.
      4. Balance atoms of oxidized/reduced species.
      5. Balance remaining atoms (H, O) using H₂O and H⁺/OH⁻ (acidic/basic medium).
      6. Verify overall charge balance.
      Example: Balance KMnO₄ + HCl → KCl + MnCl₂ + H₂O + Cl₂ (acidic medium).
      • ON of Mn in KMnO₄: +7; in MnCl₂: +2 (reduction, gain 5 e⁻ per Mn).
      • ON of Cl in HCl: -1; in Cl₂: 0 (oxidation, loss 1 e⁻ per Cl atom).
      • Equalize e⁻: 2 Mn atoms gain 10 e⁻ total; 10 Cl atoms lose 10 e⁻ total (forming 5 Cl₂).
      • Partial equation: 2KMnO₄ + ... → 2MnCl₂ + ... + 5Cl₂.
      • Balance K, Mn, Cl: 2KMnO₄ → 2KCl + 2MnCl₂ (K: 2, Mn: 2, Cl: 2+4=6). Total Cl needed: 6 (products) + 10 (oxidized) = 16 → 16HCl.
      • Balance O and H: Left O = 8 (2KMnO₄). Right O = 8H₂O (8 O). Left H⁺ = 16 (16HCl). Right H = 16 (8H₂O). Final balanced equation: 2KMnO₄ + 16HCl → 2KCl + 2MnCl₂ + 5Cl₂ + 8H₂O
    • Ion-Electron (Half-Reaction) Method:
      1. Split reaction into oxidation and reduction half-reactions.
      2. Balance atoms (excluding O, H) in each half.
      3. Balance O by adding H₂O; balance H by adding H⁺/OH⁻ (acidic/basic).
      4. Balance charge by adding electrons (e⁻).
      5. Multiply half-reactions to equalize electrons lost/gained.
      6. Combine and cancel common terms.
      Example: Balance the same reaction (KMnO₄ + HCl → products) using ion-electron method.
      • Reduction Half-Reaction (MnO₄⁻ → Mn²⁺): - Balance Mn: MnO₄⁻ → Mn²⁺. - Balance O: Add 4H₂O → MnO₄⁻ → Mn²⁺ + 4H₂O. - Balance H: Add 8H⁺ → MnO₄⁻ + 8H⁺ → Mn²⁺ + 4H₂O. - Balance charge: Left (-1 + 8) = +7; Right (+2) = +2. Add 5e⁻ to left → MnO₄⁻ + 8H⁺ + 5e⁻ → Mn²⁺ + 4H₂O (balanced).
      • Oxidation Half-Reaction (Cl⁻ → Cl₂): - Balance Cl: 2Cl⁻ → Cl₂. - Balance charge: Left (-2); Right (0). Add 2e⁻ to right → 2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻ (balanced).
      • Equalize Electrons: Reduction needs 5e⁻; oxidation produces 2e⁻. LCM(5,2)=10. Multiply reduction by 2, oxidation by 5: - Reduction (×2): 2MnO₄⁻ + 16H⁺ + 10e⁻ → 2Mn²⁺ + 8H₂O. - Oxidation (×5): 10Cl⁻ → 5Cl₂ + 10e⁻.
      • Combine: Add half-reactions → 2MnO₄⁻ + 16H⁺ + 10e⁻ + 10Cl⁻ → 2Mn²⁺ + 8H₂O + 5Cl₂ + 10e⁻. Cancel e⁻ → 2MnO₄⁻ + 16H⁺ + 10Cl⁻ → 2Mn²⁺ + 8H₂O + 5Cl₂.
      • Incorporate Dissociated Ions: KMnO₄ → K⁺ + MnO₄⁻. Add 2K⁺ to left and 2KCl to right (to balance K⁺ and Cl⁻). Final equation: 2KMnO₄ + 16HCl → 2KCl + 2MnCl₂ + 5Cl₂ + 8H₂O (matches oxidation number method result).
  • Real-life Significance:
    • Corrosion: Oxidation of metals (e.g., Iron rusting: 4Fe + 3O₂ + 6H₂O → 4Fe(OH)₃ → 2Fe₂O₃·3H₂O; Fe oxidized to Fe³⁺).
    • Respiration: Oxidation of glucose by O₂ to release energy. - Reaction: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O (O₂ is oxidizing agent, reduced to H₂O).
    • Batteries: - Lead-Acid Battery: Pb (oxidized to Pb²⁺) and PbO₂ (reduced to Pb²⁺) react with H₂SO₄. - Simplified reaction: Pb + PbO₂ + 2H₂SO₄ → 2PbSO₄ + 2H₂O.
    • Combustion: Rapid oxidation (e.g., CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O; CH₄ oxidized, O₂ reduced).
    • Biological Processes: Enzyme-catalyzed reactions (e.g., ethanol → acetic acid) and cellular respiration involve redox.
